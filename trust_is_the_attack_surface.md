# Trust Is the Attack Surface: What a Toy Markov Model Says About Poisoning In-Context Memory

A large language model has two memories. One is the weights — slow, expensive, frozen at deployment, holding everything the model "knows." The other is the context window — fast, free, rewritten every turn, holding what the model has been *told* right now. In-context learning is the model leaning on that second memory, and it is most of what makes these systems feel adaptive: you paste in a document, a style, a set of instructions, and the model bends to it without a single weight changing.

Anything a model leans on, an attacker can lean on too. So here is the natural worry, and it is a clean one: **the more usefully a model relies on its context, the more an attacker who poisons that context can steer it.** Usefulness and exploitability would then be two names for one thing — the model's dependence on what it was just told. Call it the conservation hypothesis. It is intuitive, it is the kind of thing you'd put in a threat model without checking, and as far as I can tell it is wrong.

This piece is about what replaces it. I built a deliberately stripped-down model where the question has an exact answer, asked it directly, and watched the clean hypothesis fail — in a way that points at something more useful. The short version: exploitability does not track usefulness. It tracks **trust** — how much weight the model places on a piece of context — and trust and usefulness are not the same quantity. They coincide in one regime and come apart in another, and the regime where they come apart is the dangerous one, because there the model is most poisonable exactly where its context is doing it the least good.

I'll state the finding on the toy, then spend the back half of the article on the conjecture it suggests for real transformers, which is the thing I actually want to go test.

## Why a toy, and why a cipher

You cannot ask this question cleanly of a real transformer. The quantities you'd want — how far a single poisoned token's influence propagates, where in the network that influence can hide, how much a perturbation is amplified — are either approximate or flatly uncomputable in a model with attention in it. Attention mixes content-dependently: the thing doing the mixing is itself a function of the input you're perturbing, so there's no fixed object to analyze. Every bound you'd write down has an unbounded constant or a sum over astronomically many paths you can't enumerate.

So drop to a model that keeps the one feature under study and throws away everything else. The structure I care about is *two memories, linearly combined*: a frozen "parametric" model standing in for the weights, plus an online model estimated live from the current sequence standing in for the context window, mixed together to make each prediction. There's a clean pedagogical version of exactly this — a global Markov model augmented by an online Markov "cache," built as a from-scratch analogue of the parametric-vs-in-context split (the lineage here is Bietti et al.'s *Birth of a Transformer*). The cache warms up as a sequence streams in; that warm-up *is* in-context learning, made of nothing but counts.

The reason to read poisoning through a cryptographic lens is that injecting a crafted input to steer an output is, structurally, a **differential attack**: you introduce a difference at the input and ask how it propagates to the output. And here's the payoff of going to a Markov model specifically — in a literal Markov mixture, the objects from differential cryptanalysis that are only ever *approximations* for a cipher (or a transformer) become **exact**. The "Markov cipher" assumption that lets cryptanalysts multiply per-round probabilities is normally a heuristic; here it holds by construction, because the thing genuinely is a Markov chain. The worst-case per-step decay of a difference is the chain's Dobrushin coefficient, in closed form. The "differential hull" — the sum over all the ways a difference can propagate — is a resolvent you can actually invert. And the leverage of a single poisoned count in a context seen `n` times is exactly `1/(n+a)`, plain estimator variance. The toy isn't a cartoon of the analysis; it's the place where the analysis is real. That is the entire reason it earns its keep.

## The experiment

The synthetic source has a dial I'll call `pi_ctx`: the fraction of the sequence driven by per-document structure that the global model provably cannot have learned (each document gets its own private regularities, which wash out across the training corpus). So `pi_ctx` is a clean knob on *how much genuine in-context information exists* — how much there is for the cache to usefully grab, and equally, how much an attacker can hope to exploit. Turn it up and context matters more.

Across that dial I measured four things. **Usefulness**: bits per symbol saved by using the cache versus the global model alone. **Trust**: how much weight the combined prediction places on the cache, the warmed-up reliance. And two flavors of **exploitability** — a one-shot version (inject a single count, measure how much it moves the very next prediction) and a propagated version (inject a single count once, then let the sequence keep streaming, and accumulate how much that one injection bends every later prediction). The distinction between those two turns out to matter more than I expected.

## Result one: the obvious exploitability measure is the wrong one

The one-shot measure — how much can a single injected count move the next prediction — is what most people would reach for, and it is anti-correlated with usefulness (around −0.95 across the sweep). It actually *falls* as context becomes more useful, because a useful, warmed-up context has high counts, and one extra count out of many barely moves a thing (that `1/(n+a)` again). One-shot leverage is a red herring.

The propagated measure is the honest one, and it maps exactly onto a cryptographic instinct: the strength of a differential attack is never one trail, it's the **hull** — the accumulated effect over every path the difference can take. A poison that doesn't propagate is cheap everywhere; a poison that does is the whole game. Once you measure propagated leverage instead of one-shot leverage, the picture sharpens — and the clean conservation hypothesis dies.

## Result two: exploitability tracks trust, not usefulness

Here is the failure, and the replacement. With a low-order cache — few possible contexts, so the cache fills up no matter what — trust **saturates**. The reliance sits near 0.9 across the entire `pi_ctx` sweep, whether or not the cache is helping. And propagated exploitability tracks that flat, saturated trust: it stays high even at the low end of the dial, where the cache is actively *hurting* the model by more than a bit per symbol. The correlation between usefulness and exploitability collapses to about 0.5. **The model is fully poisonable precisely where its context is useless.**

That sentence is the article. Usefulness and exploitability are not one quantity. The quantity that exploitability actually follows is trust — the weight the model puts on the context — and trust can be high while usefulness is zero or negative. The cache that has filled up with counts is trusted because it has *data*, not because that data is *good*, and the linear mixture hands it weight on the basis of quantity, not quality. A poisoner doesn't need the context to be useful. They need it to be trusted.

## Result three: when trust and usefulness do coincide

They are not always decoupled, and the condition that couples them is the interesting part. With a higher-order cache — many possible contexts, most of them rare — trust has to be *earned*. A high-order context only accumulates enough counts to be trusted if it actually recurs, and in this source a context recurring is itself evidence of genuine local structure. In that regime usefulness, trust, and propagated exploitability all rise together, tightly: the correlation between usefulness and exploitability is about 0.97. Here the conservation hypothesis is true.

So the law is real but **conditional**, and the condition is *context sparsity*. Where contexts are dense and trust is handed out for mere quantity of data, trust decouples from usefulness and the model is exploitable independent of benefit. Where contexts are sparse and trust must be earned from real recurrence, trust and usefulness move together and exploitability is, in effect, paid for by capability. The same architecture sits in both regimes depending only on how sparse its contexts are.

## Result four: condensation

One more thing falls out, and it's the part that should make you nervous. In *generation*, a model samples a symbol and then conditions on what it just produced — a feedback loop. Under high trust that loop is positively reinforcing, which makes it a nonlinear Pólya urn, and nonlinear Pólya urns have a **condensation transition**: below a threshold the system stays diffuse, above it a single early advantage locks in and takes over. Seed one self-reinforcing poison into the context and sweep the trust the model places on it: takeover of the generated output stays near zero up to a reliance of about 0.9, then turns sharply upward, past 0.6 once reliance crosses 0.99. A single injected element is shrugged off when trust is moderate and **locks in** once trust is high enough. Poisoning in-context memory is not necessarily a graded thing; above the knee it's a phase change.

## The conjecture

The toy is a toy. But it makes a sharp enough claim that the analogous statement for real transformers is worth naming, and naming it precisely is the point of this piece — I want to use it to organize a real research program.

**The trust-saturation conjecture.** *A transformer's in-context exploitability — how much poisoning the context window steers downstream generation — is governed by how much the model trusts the relevant context (the effective weight its computation places on it), not by how much that context helps the task. Consequently: (a) where context-reliance is earned from genuine, sparse structure, as in most natural-language prediction, trust and usefulness coincide and poisoning is roughly bounded by benefit; but (b) there exist context regimes where trust saturates independent of usefulness, and in those regimes a model is maximally poisonable exactly where its context contributes least, with a condensation-like threshold above which a single injected element can take over generation.*

There's a real reason to think transformers may often live in the safe regime (a), and it's the most direct bridge from the toy: high-order natural-language contexts are sparse — most long prefixes are nearly unique — which is the earned-trust regime. The repo now includes a small `demo_real_text_cache.py` calibration on committed sample prose, but that is still the toy substrate consuming text, not a transformer measurement. If the earned-trust story survives real-model tests, in-context exploitability is paid for by in-context capability, which is a reassuring trade.

The conjecture's bite is regime (b): the prediction that **trust-saturation regimes exist and are findable**, and that they are exactly the dangerous ones. The prime suspect is induction — the copy mechanism. An induction head trusts a context span because it *matches* a previous span, not because copying it helps; the toy's longest-match predictor is a deliberately simple surrogate for that copy behavior, and it earns trust on grounds orthogonal to benefit. So the conjecture predicts that **induction-dominated, highly repetitive, or heavily templated contexts are where trust most decouples from usefulness, and where a single planted token is most likely to undergo lock-in.** Repeated boilerplate, structured formats, long verbatim quotes, retrieval dumps with duplicated passages — these are the places to look, and the prediction is specific: poisonability there should be high and roughly *independent* of how much the repeated content actually informs the task.

That turns into concrete experiments. Build a transformer analogue of propagated leverage — influence of a poisoned context token on downstream generation, via attention-based influence or activation patching — and measure it against an analogue of trust (effective attention weight on that token) and against task benefit, swept from natural prose to maximally repetitive input. The conjecture predicts leverage tracks the trust axis and not the benefit axis, and that the gap between them opens up as you move toward induction-dominated inputs. Probe for the condensation threshold directly: plant one adversarial token in a repetitive context and measure takeover of generation as a function of attention weight or sampling temperature, looking for the knee. The toy-on-text calibration is only a preliminary check; the load-bearing claim is the transformer experiment.

The defensive implication is already legible from the toy, and it inverts the obvious instinct. If you're hardening a model against context poisoning, the quantity to watch and to bound is **trust, not usefulness** — and specifically trust saturation in dense or repetitive contexts, because a context can be fully trusted while contributing nothing, and that is the exact configuration an attacker wants. A safe-by-default policy should cap how much weight any context span can earn on the basis of sheer recurrence, independent of whether that span is pulling its weight on the task. Tuning defenses against measured usefulness would protect precisely the wrong regime.

## What I'm claiming and what I'm not

The conservation hypothesis I started with — usefulness equals exploitability — was falsified by its own experiment, and I think that's the most trustworthy way to arrive at the replacement. What survived is narrower and stranger: exploitability follows trust, trust and usefulness only coincide under context sparsity, and dense-context trust saturation is a poisonability regime hiding in plain sight. All of that is established *on the toy*, exactly, with the cryptographic objects made literal rather than approximate. None of it is yet measured on a transformer. The bridge — that real LLMs often occupy earned-trust regimes but have findable saturation regimes around induction and repetition — is a conjecture, stated above so it can be attacked. I'd rather put it on the table sharply and be wrong in a specific way than hedge it into something unfalsifiable. The experiments that would settle it are not exotic; they're the obvious next thing, and they're what I'm proposing to do.
